Services in Oxford

Your GP

If you’re a student at Oxford and you’re worried about your mental wellbeing, you may want to make an appointment with a doctor to discuss it. You should be registered with your college’s practice, but you can re-register with any practice in Oxford if you want to.

GPs in Oxford tend to have a lot of experience dealing with student mental wellbeing. They make a good port of call, as they have an overview of the different services available across Oxford and they know what the student experience is like. They’ll be able to give you a better understanding of what you’re going through, and tell you what your options are.

They might recommend adjustments to lifestyle that may help – such as exercise, or sleep – or, if it is needed, they can prescribe medicine. They are bound by strict rules of doctor-patient confidentiality, but if you want them to, they can talk to your tutors on their behalf about your situation.

College Nurse

http://www.ox.ac.uk/students/shw/health/doctors/collegedoctors

Like your GP, college nurses have a good overview of the resources available and considerable experience of helping students with a range of concerns. College nurses have daily clinics during term-time, and can also speak to tutors on your behalf, as well as offering regular support in times of need.

Oxford University Counselling Service

http://ox.ac.uk/students/shw/counselling

The university counselling service offers individual counselling, group counselling and workshops. The website has links to self-help resources and advice for those who find themselves supporting someone through a mental health problem.

If you sign up for counselling, you will be offered an initial one-hour session designed to assess your situation and your needs, and to talk about how you might move forward. This might be all you need, but it is common to have a number of additional sessions. You might then be referred to one of the Service’s group sessions or workshops.

The Counselling Service also has a very comprehensive list of self-help resources that can be found here.

Oxford University Mental Health Mentoring

http://www.ox.ac.uk/media/global/wwwoxacuk/localsites/studentgateway/documents/disabilityadvisoryservice/Mentor_information_sheet.pdf

If your mental health problem is long-standing, you may be eligible for mentoring support from the Disability Advisory Service. Mentors can help students to maintain study patterns, and assist with timetabling, goal setting, and managing self-imposed expectation, with the aim of providing every student with an equal opportunity to realise their academic potential. Mentoring can be accessed by students for a number of conditions, such as depression, anxiety, eating disorders, schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, obsessive compulsive disorder and psychosis.

If you have any questions, don’t hesitate to get in touch with the Disability Advisory Service, who can be contacted at disability@admin.ox.ac.uk.

Oxford University Peer Support

http://www.ox.ac.uk/students/shw/peer/#d.en.30715

Peer Supporters are undergraduate and graduate students of the University who have received training to enable them to listen effectively, communicate sensitively, maintain confidentiality, respect boundaries and recognise when and how to encourage referral to professional support services. Peer Support offers an easily accessible and relatively informal opportunity to talk through issues which may be concerning you. Often it can help simply to get things off your chest or to know that someone is genuinely willing to listen and take time to understand what’s on your mind. Sometimes just talking things through is enough; sometimes it may lead you to seek more professional help.

Peer Supporters are college based and contact details can be found by emailing Welfare Representatives or on posters around colleges. Be aware that whilst most do, not all colleges have a Peer Support programme running.

Student Minds

http://studentminds.org.uk

Oxford 'Student Minds' Eating Disorder Group, meets weekly through out term to support students with eating disorders. The group is run by a team of trained student volunteers, making the meetings friendly and relaxed. Student Minds aims to bring students together to share strategies for managing mental health, to talk honestly in a safe and pro-recovery environment. While anyone is welcome to attend, our groups always have a pro-recovery focus. Each session has a suggested discussion topic, such as settling into university or talking to friends, but you are very welcome to bring along any topic of your own which you would like to discuss. There is no need to sign up, just drop by and say hello! For more information, please visit the Student Minds website or email oxford@studentminds.org.uk.

Archway Foundation

http://archway.moonfruit.com

Loneliness is an experience many of us may go through at various points in our lives. Feelings of loneliness affect people of all ages and backgrounds. The Archway Foundation was established in Oxford in 1982 to help relieve some of the real distress caused by loneliness. We connect those feeling alienated with a diverse group of volunteers, many of whom know for themselves how damaging loneliness can be.
